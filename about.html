Background

The full story

I started my career writing code and managing servers for a small SaaS company in India — before "DevOps" was even a job title. The term for what I was doing then was simply "making it work", and I've been doing that ever since, at increasing scale and complexity.

Over 15 years, I've grown from hands-on infrastructure engineer to the person organisations trust with their entire cloud strategy. The trajectory runs through SOC 2 audits at fintech startups, Kubernetes migrations for global consumer brands serving millions of users, ISO 27001 certifications that unlocked enterprise contracts, and end-to-end platform builds that went from whiteboard to production in under six months.

Today, I sit at the intersection of architecture, security, and organisational impact. I don't just build platforms — I build the foundations that let engineering teams move fast, operate reliably, and scale without friction. My work at keylight GmbH is the clearest expression of this: sole infrastructure authority for a FinTech startup, accountable for every cloud decision from first principles to production compliance.

What drives me is the compounding effect of good platform decisions. When you get the infrastructure right — the abstractions, the standards, the security posture, the observability — everything above it gets easier. Engineering teams ship faster. The organisation takes on more ambitious work. Security becomes a lever for growth rather than a blocker. That's the kind of infrastructure leadership I've built a career doing, and what I want to do at the largest possible scale.

I'm moving toward engineering leadership — Director or Staff / Principal Engineer scope — where architectural thinking and organisational impact operate together, and where the right infrastructure decisions shape entire product trajectories, not just individual services.

How I think

Engineering philosophy

01 — Platform thinking
Infrastructure is a product, not a service

The platform team's customers are the engineering teams. That means platform decisions need to be evaluated on developer experience, cognitive load, and the velocity they enable — not just technical elegance. I treat internal infrastructure with the same product discipline as customer-facing software.

02 — Security posture
Security that enables, not security that blocks

Bad security is security that slows things down without a clear return. Good security is invisible in day-to-day flow and shows up as confidence — the confidence to take on bigger customers, move faster, and ship without fear. I design security controls as multipliers, not gates.

03 — Reliability
Heroics are a platform failure

If your organisation needs heroes to keep the lights on, the platform has failed. Good infrastructure is boring infrastructure — it runs quietly, recovers automatically, and gives engineers confidence to ship on a Friday. I design for the absence of incidents, not just recovery from them.

04 — Decision-making
Reversibility over optimality

In fast-moving environments, the best architecture decision is often the one that's easiest to change. I favour reversible choices, explicit interfaces, and abstractions that let teams evolve their services without platform lock-in — even when a tighter coupling would look cleaner on paper today.

05 — Compliance
Compliance as a commercial lever

ISO 27001, PCI-DSS, SOC 2 — these aren't regulatory hurdles, they're business development tools. The organisations I've worked with that treated compliance as a strategic investment rather than an audit cost consistently opened doors their less security-mature competitors couldn't.

06 — Leadership
Leadership is about making yourself unnecessary

The goal of a good engineering leader isn't to be indispensable — it's to build teams and systems that are stronger than any individual. I invest heavily in documentation, on-call runbooks, architectural decision records, and the kind of knowledge transfer that means no one person holds the keys.
